# How we work

We move fast — not frantic, but fast in the way that comes from clear priorities, good tools, and a bias toward shipping. We're an async team spread across time zones: everyone sets their own schedule, and we're measured by what we deliver, not by hours at a desk. This page covers both the mindset we work by and the practices that keep us aligned.

## Our operating principles

1. **Outcomes are the score.** Success is measured by what moves, not by how long someone sat with it. A problem solved in two hours is worth every bit as much as one solved in twenty — often more. A full week logged means nothing if nothing shipped. Value delivered is the scoreboard.

2. **Outcomes and honest time, together.** Time and outcomes answer different questions — "what did it cost?" and "what did we create?" — and we read them together, never one alone. Because we use live timers rather than after-the-fact timesheets (see [Time Tracking](#time-tracking)), honesty is automatic and finishing fast is rewarded rather than hidden. Track the truth; the numbers only help us if they're real.

3. **Ship something every day.** Every workday should end with visible progress, and a rough working slice today beats a polished, invisible one next week — 20% delivered now is worth more than 100% delivered "soon." This is why we send daily updates (see [Client / Project Updates](#client--project-updates)): if a day ends with nothing to show, that's a signal to raise early, not to delay the reveal.

4. **Cut the work down until it fits.** The most valuable skill on the team is turning a boulder into pebbles. When a task is too big for a day, carve off the piece that can be finished, ship it, state plainly what's left, and prioritize the rest together. "It's all one thing, it'll be delivered when it's done" is the habit to unlearn — almost nothing is truly all-or-nothing.

5. **Speed is part of quality.** These aren't opposites to trade between. Fast-and-good is the bar; slow-and-good is a warning sign; slow-and-buggy is a real problem. Don't over-polish before sharing — early feedback beats a perfect first draft, because often the perfect draft was solving the wrong thing.

6. **Use AI like it's the job — because it is.** Reach for agents, skills, and internal tooling first. Grinding through something manually when a tool already does it isn't diligence, it's lost time. Use AI to pressure-test work before it's handed over, too — catching issues early is the expectation. Moving slowly with the tools switched off reads as underperformance.

7. **Solve it once, then never again.** When someone figures something out, they don't just fix it — they capture it as a reusable skill, agent, or doc so the next person (including their future self) inherits the answer for free. Leverage compounds only if solutions stick; re-solving the same problem twice is the tax we exist to eliminate.

8. **Communicate early, not perfectly.** Share what you're seeing before it's wrapped up — drafts, blockers, "here's why this is stuck," "here's what won't get done today." Early and rough is a gift; silence until it's perfect is the real failure mode.

9. **Bring urgency by default.** Treat every task like it matters now, because it usually does. The question isn't "when is this due?" but "how good can this be, how fast?" We set our own hours — but while working, we work with focus.

### What this does not mean

Because "move fast" gets misread, to be exact:

* **Fast ≠ reckless.** Speed comes from good judgment and good tooling, not from skipping thinking. Cutting scope is smart; cutting corners on something that ships to a user is not.

* **De-scoping ≠ abandoning quality.** The slice that ships should be genuinely done, not a facade. Smaller, but real.

* **Tracking time ≠ rewarding time.** The timers, metrics, and AI analysis are about honesty and capacity, not time-at-desk. We never celebrate longer hours — we celebrate outcomes. Live tracking exists so fast people get automatic credit for being fast, and so nobody quietly burns 20 hours on something we'd have gladly cut. We read time and output together; both a timer left running and a silent 20-hour grind show up, and both are failures — raise them early instead.

* **This is not a burnout culture.** The intensity is about focus and pace while working, not about who's online at 2am. Async freedom is real, and sustainability matters — we'd rather have people sharp for a year than sprinting for a month.

* **"Use AI" ≠ shipping whatever it produces.** You own the output. AI accelerates you; it doesn't absolve you. Verify before handing it over.

### In short

> Outcomes are the score — a 2-hour win beats a 20-hour slog. We track time with live timers, read alongside output and AI analysis, so honesty is automatic and speed is rewarded. Ship daily. Cut work down until it fits a day. Speed and quality are the same goal. Use AI aggressively — and verify it. Capture every solution as something reusable. Communicate early and rough. Default to urgency, never recklessness.

## Async

* We mostly work asynchronically, which means that you are free to work at any hour you prefer! Our employees & contractors live in different time zones.

## Client / Project Updates

* Unless our client/project requires more frequent updates (e.g. hourly), as a standard, all our employees should provide daily updates about the progress.

* If the project follows Scrum, it's best, of course, to make it over a daily standup meeting.

* If the project did not follow Scrum, we found that it's best to provide 2 updates to the client per day: one when an employee starts work and notifies the client about the plan to work on specific tasks/features and a second notification right before employee finish work, about progress on tasks/features done during the workday.

* Every update should include videos and/or screenshots about features that were built (if it's appropriate of course) to get early feedback and reference tasks numbers (e.g. task URL in JIRA or Github issue # / URL, etc., of course, if the project uses some sort of tasks management tool)

* If an employee is going to take a day off, it’s mandatory to notify the client in advance.

## Time Tracking

* All our employees/contractors are using Time Tracking tools (e.g. http://github.com/ever-co/ever-gauzy, Upwork Timer, HubStaff timer, or some other timer software client prefer to use for a particular project)

* We rely on live timers that you start and stop as you work — not manually entered timesheets. The record reflects real work as it happens, so there is nothing to fill in later or round up.

* We read time data together with delivered output and AI-assisted analysis. Time speaks to capacity and honesty; output speaks to value. Neither is judged on its own.

* Because timers are live, working fast is visibly rewarded — a story finished in an hour reads as an hour. The only thing that breaks this is a timer left running while you are not actually on the task.

* Nobody controls the time of day when our employees/contractors work - they are free to set their own schedule as long as it allows them to do one short daily meeting (only if required by client project). Of course, depending on the project and the client you work for, additional calls/chats/meetings may need to be scheduled.
